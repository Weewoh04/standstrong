POTS 101

Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome (POTS) is a form of dysautonomia where heart rate rises excessively on standing, often with dizziness, fatigue, brain fog, and exercise intolerance.

Quick Definition

  • Heart rate rise of 30+ BPM within 10 minutes of standing in adults
  • Symptoms worsen upright and improve when lying down
  • Common triggers: heat, dehydration, long standing, illness, stress

What Is POTS?

POTS is a dysautonomia condition where your autonomic nervous system struggles to regulate blood flow and heart rate when upright. In practice, this often means your body compensates with a rapid pulse to keep blood moving to your brain.

Symptoms can vary day to day and person to person. Some people are mostly limited by fatigue and brain fog, while others experience severe orthostatic intolerance and frequent symptom flares.

Common Symptoms

Cardiovascular

Palpitations, rapid heart rate, shortness of breath, chest discomfort.

Neurologic

Lightheadedness, fainting or near-fainting, headaches, brain fog.

Energy

Post-exertional crashes, poor stamina, severe daytime fatigue.

GI/Other

Nausea, bloating, temperature sensitivity, sleep disruption.

Core Management Pillars

  1. Fluids: build daily hydration consistency, not just reactive intake.
  2. Sodium: follow clinician guidance for increased salt when appropriate.
  3. Compression: waist-high or abdominal-inclusive compression helps blood return.
  4. Pacing: avoid push-crash cycles by planning recovery windows.
  5. Exercise: start recumbent, progress slowly, track heart rate response.

Exercise Principles for POTS

  • Start in low-orthostatic positions: lying down, recumbent bike, rowing, water-based work.
  • Use progression phases: recumbent to seated to standing over weeks to months.
  • Increase one variable at a time: duration, then frequency, then intensity.
  • Stop and recover if symptoms escalate quickly or HR spikes past your safety plan.
  • Consistency beats intensity. Small sessions done regularly are most effective.
Safety note Exercise programming for POTS should be individualized. Work with your cardiologist, autonomic specialist, or PT when possible.
